Daily Mind Work In Progress

Daily activities to improve mental health.

Overview

"Daily Mind” is an activity based experience where users complete 3 activities each day aimed at improving mental health. It aims to be an activity-based, free-to-access cross-platform mental health app that has low time commitment, effective at improving the mental health of users and limited barriers to usee.

Project Details

Purpose: Personal project

Role: UX/UI Design, literature research, motion design and user interviews.

Tools: Adobe Illustrator, Adobe After Effects, Figma and Protopie.

Problem space

A cross-sectional study by Salahedin and Mason (2016, p.6-7), indicated that of the young adults in the U.K. experiencing poor mental health, many chose not to access mental health care. Over 67% of young adults reported, financial cost as a barrier to accessing mental health care. Similarly, 81.4% respondents reported feelings of shame associated with accessing mental health care.

This made me ask...

How might I improve mental wellbeing for young adults?

Discovery Phase

What pain points were associated with managing mental health?

Research round 1: Identifying opportunities

I interviewed 3 people and surveyed 7 people within my target group of 20-29 year olds to understand when people felt they felt connected to eachother. I did this research after reading an article by Klussman et. al. (2021) whose findings indicated that peoples sense of connection to themselves and to others are strong indicators of mental health. This helped me understand the problem to a greater degree.

Exploratory Wireframes

I explored how a website could potentially address this issue. I created a variety of wireframes to outline how the website might look, what activities it might include, how much learning information vs. activities it might include and other factors.

Testing, feedback and reflection

Following rounds of testing, feedback and reflection on these, I realised that these weren't the right solution, as users usually needed to be friends with another person to get the mental health benefits from experiencing connection with another person. Also, creating new activities to improve mental health was a big undertaking, and was likely out of scope and would likely be inneffective, as there are many existing activities which affect mental health positively which are backed up by mental health literature. I felt the need to do more research to understand the problem a bit better before proceeding.

Research round 2: Understanding user pain points

Following this round of research, I desired to understand user pain points to a more significant degree. I conducted a further 4 interviews and 6 surveys in order to help me discover user contexts as well as user pain points.

Early concept testing

At the end of each interview/survey, I also tested 2 concept ideas with the participants to help get a feel of what might be more effective.

Concept 1: Connect!

A 1 month long program you do in conjunction with a friend to foster connection with eachother and take ownership of your mental health together.

Each day comprises of

A call with one another (or in-person chat).
Completing an evidence-based activity (e.g. Journalling).
Learning about a mental health practice together. A sharing session.

Intended outcomes

Learn more about yourselves.
Become better friends.
Get mental clarity.
Understand and realign your personal goals a bit better.

Concept 2: 30 Day Mind

A free-to-access daily evidence-based mental health activity pack website.

Each day comprises of

Completing a relaxing game.
Completing an evidence-based activity (e.g. Journalling).
Reading about practical mental health practices to incorporate (eg. water, exercise, probiotics).

Intended outcomes

Improve mental health flexibly in your own time.
Learn and do activities in a casual manner to encourage continuous mental health improvement.

Findings

From this I discovered that concept 2 was much more effective. Users were much more interested in a solution that they didn't have to do with a friend as they foresaw a lot of things not being well navigated in concept 1. They were much more interested in having something they could use flexibly in their own time.

Competitor analysis

Organisations such as The Black Dog Institute (2022), Headspace (2022) and the Calm App (2022) provide different methods of treating mental health through digital apps. The Apps Calm and Headspace were the largest players in the market with the most downloads comparatively. They are only focused on providing meditation and mindfulness activities. Calm also provides a short wellness course with a subscription. They are both paid apps. Alternatively, The Black Dog Institute has an App which is still in testing for Australian University Students and is not yet released to the broader public. Their existing program, myCompass, is a 7-week learning program, but was negatively reviewed for its inability to provide "engaging features," as well as continuous use of text and lack of engaging imagery (MindToolsIo, 2016). Moodfit (2022) is another app with various mental health tools, however for this app users are required to put together a regime for themselves through various tools which could hinder return usage.

Prototype

I began prototyping an idea for the website which provided a once-a-day user flow for users comprised of a short animation when users arrived at the site, an evidence-based activity and a short information section section where users could casually learn about evidence based mental health tools to improve their wellbeing. I started by mapping out what each day might look like, then proceeding to prototype it.

Migration to protopie

Unfortunately, for this particular project Figma's prototyping tools were not advanced enough and would require too much explanation to the user during testing, rather than providing an accurate portrayal of what a user flow might feel like. For this reason I decided to migrate my project to protopie.

Testing and user feedback

Upon conducting testing, several key things were discovered.

  • Users struggled with how broad the journalling activity was.
  • Users enjoyed the gratitude and breathing exercise.
  • Users wanted the option to select their own activities and many didn't notice the "Change Activity" button.
  • Some users found the context of what they were doing a bit unclear.
  • Users thought they would be more likely to use this if it were an app.
  • Users claimed that the animations were anti-climactic on occasion.
