Daily Mind

Daily activities to improve mental health.

Overview

"Daily Mind‚Äù is an activity based experience where users complete 3 activities each day aimed at improving mental health. It aims to be a free-to-access cross-platform mental health app that has low time commitment, is effective at improving the mental health of users and has limited barriers to usee.

Project Details

Purpose: Personal project

Tasks: UX/UI Design, literature research, competitor analysis, motion design, graphic design, illustration and user interviews.

Tools: Adobe Illustrator, Adobe After Effects, Figma and Protopie.

Project Motivation

I was initially motivated to do this project as I was previously on a panel helping to interview new youth peer educators at my previous workplace. One of the questions we asked was "What prominent issues do you think young people are currently facing?" Of the 4 interviewees, all of them noted youth mental health as a major factor. This made me consider the issue within my own networks and community.

Further problem research

I began by reading about the problem. I found that a cross-sectional study by Salahedin and Mason (2016), indicated that of the young adults in the U.K. experiencing poor mental health, many chose not to access mental health care. Over 67% of young adults reported, financial cost as a barrier to accessing mental health care. Similarly, 81.4% respondents reported feelings of shame associated with accessing mental health care.

This made me ask...

How might I improve mental wellbeing for young adults?

Discovery Phase

What pain points were associated with managing mental health?

Competitor analysis

Organisations such as The Black Dog Institute, Headspace and the Calm App provided different methods of treating mental health through digital apps. The Apps Calm and Headspace were the largest players in the market with the most downloads comparatively however their main offering was providing meditation and mindfulness activities. They are both paid apps. Alternatively, The Black Dog Institute has an App which is still in testing for Australian University Students and is not yet released to the broader public. Their existing program, myCompass, is a 7-week learning program, but was negatively reviewed for its inability to provide "engaging features," as well as continuous use of text and lack of engaging imagery. Moodfit is another app with various mental health tools, however for this app users are required to put together a regime for themselves through various tools which could hinder return usage. I identified a gap in the market for an activity-based free-to-access cross-platform mental health app that has lower time commitment, and is easier to set up and get going with.

I documented my findings in a spreadsheet.

Research round 1: Identifying opportunities

I interviewed 3 people (over the phone and in-person) and surveyed 7 people within my target group of 20-29 year olds to understand when people felt they felt connected to eachother. I did this research after reading an article by Klussman et. al. (2021) whose findings indicated that peoples sense of connection to themselves and to others are strong indicators of mental health. I wanted to explore this notion.

The survey had 13 responses however several were filtered out as they weren't the demographic.

Exploratory Wireframes

I explored how a website could potentially address this issue. I created a variety of wireframes to outline how the website might look, what activities it might include, how much learning information vs. activities it might include and other factors.

Testing, feedback and reflection

At this stage I showed the prototype to potential users and my UX mentor. I realised that these weren't the right solution, as users usually needed to be friends with another person to get the mental health benefits from experiencing connection with another person. Also, creating new activities to improve mental health was a large undertaking, and was likely out of scope and would likely be inneffective, as there are many existing activities which affect mental health positively which are backed up by mental health literature. I needed to gather more data points and understand the problem further.

Research round 2: Understanding user pain points

Following this round of research, I desired to understand user pain points to a more significant degree. I conducted a further 4 interviews and 6 surveys in order to help me discover user contexts as well as user pain points.

I received 6 responses within my desired demographic from surveys.

Early concept testing

At the end of each interview/survey, I also ran through 2 concept ideas with the participants to help get a feel of what might be more effective.

Concept 1: Connect!

A 1 month long program you do in conjunction with a friend to foster connection with eachother and take ownership of your mental health together.

Each day comprises of

A call with one another (or in-person chat).
Completing an evidence-based activity (e.g. Journalling).
Learning about a mental health practice together. A sharing session.

Intended outcomes

Learn more about yourselves.
Become better friends.
Get mental clarity.
Understand and realign your personal goals a bit better.

Concept 2: 30 Day Mind

A free-to-access daily evidence-based mental health activity pack website.

Each day comprises of

Completing a relaxing game.
Completing an evidence-based activity (e.g. Journalling).
Reading about practical mental health practices to incorporate (eg. water, exercise, probiotics).

Intended outcomes

Improve mental health flexibly in your own time.
Learn and do activities in a casual manner to encourage continuous mental health improvement.

Findings

From this I discovered that concept 2 was much more effective. Users were much more interested in a solution that they didn't have to do with a friend as they foresaw a lot of things not being well navigated in concept 1. They were much more interested in having something they could use flexibly in their own time.

Prototype

I began prototyping an idea for the website which provided a once-a-day user flow for users comprised of a short animation when users arrived at the site, an evidence-based activity and a short information section section where users could casually learn about evidence based mental health tools to improve their wellbeing. I started by mapping out what each day might look like, then proceeding to prototype it.

Migration to protopie

Unfortunately, for this particular project Figma's prototyping tools were not advanced enough and would require too much explanation to the user during testing, rather than providing an accurate portrayal of what a user flow might feel like. For this reason I decided to migrate my project to protopie.

Testing and user feedback

Upon conducting testing, several key things were discovered.

  • Users struggled with how broad the journalling activity was.
  • Users wanted the option to select their own activities and many didn't notice the "Change Activity" button.
  • Some users found the context of what they were doing a bit unclear.
  • Users thought they would be more likely to use this if it were an app.

Further testing

I conducted testing with 3 activities. This round of testing indicated good results with 8 out of 10 people said they would use something like this.

Common themes:

  • Some participants felt that some activities needed a bit more or could be extended a bit.
  • All participants skipped the onboarding part and then asked me what the app was about.
  • Some participants were interested in the research some weren't.

For this round of testing, I asked users to complete an activity and proceeded by asking them questions about their experience and observing any features of their journey.

Final iteration: activities which respond to a range of emotions and needs

Unwind activity: user desires to relax and clear their head

Indoors activity: user desires to exercise but not leave the house

Reflection

The Daily Mind project has been a great project which explored the potential for an ecompassing digital mental health activity pack. If this project were to be developed further, greater investment would need to be made into limiting risks associated with its use. For instance, might any of the activities cause more distress to certain groups of users. Additionally, an app such as this could benefit from further consultation by mental health professionals and their expertise would be keystone in its development.

As well as this, features which encourage continued use would be added. These include: gamification through awards - perhaps the illustrations could be collected by users, a record of the daily learnings users receive and the ability to go back and read through these, and also notifications which encourage users throughout the day might be appropriate.

Overall, this project has laid the foundation for what could likely be a valuable resource/tool for certain groups and aimed to provide a more pleasant/relevant user experience than many similar applications currently on the market. Through its implementation, individuals could have greater opportunity to improve their mental health and experience greater wellbeing.
